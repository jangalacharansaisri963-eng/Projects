# Stoichiometry

Stoichiometry is the calculation of quantities of substances involved in chemical reactions.

It is based on the idea that chemical reactions follow fixed ratios.

A balanced chemical equation works like a recipe.

For example:

2 bread + 1 cheese → 1 grilled cheese

This means:

- 2 slices of bread are required for 1 grilled cheese.
- 1 cheese is required for 1 grilled cheese.


If we want to make 7 grilled cheese sandwiches, we can calculate the required bread:

\[
7\text{ grilled cheese}
\times
\frac{2\text{ bread}}{1\text{ grilled cheese}}
\]

The grilled cheese units cancel:

\[
7 \times 2
\]

The answer is:

\[
14\text{ bread}
\]


Chemistry works in the same way.

A balanced chemical equation provides the mole ratio.

Example:

\[
2H_2+O_2\rightarrow2H_2O
\]

The equation tells us:

2 moles of hydrogen react with 1 mole of oxygen to produce 2 moles of water.


The general stoichiometry formula is:

\[
\text{Required amount}
=
\text{Given amount}
\times
\frac{\text{Required coefficient}}
{\text{Given coefficient}}
\]


The common stoichiometry steps are:

1. Convert the given quantity into moles if needed.
2. Use the mole ratio from the balanced equation.
3. Convert the result into the required unit.


Stoichiometry can be used with:

- Moles
- Mass
- Gas volume
- Number of particles


In Chemistry Calculator, stoichiometry commands use coefficients from balanced equations.

Example:

stoich_moles(7,1,2)

This means:

7 moles of a substance with coefficient 1 are converted into a substance with coefficient 2.

The result is:

14 moles


Stoichiometry is one of the most important chemistry skills because it allows us to predict how much reactant is needed or how much product can be produced.
